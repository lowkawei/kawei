‘It’s easy to fail, it’s very hard to fail like this...Catwoman sucks mightily on every level’

Kapow! comics come to ife

As the flood of comic-book films spreads, like a swarm of mutant beasts across the earth, Melanie Sheridan separates the good guys from the bad.

‘Fabulously, deliriously, egregiously awful... it’s easy to fail, it’s very hard to fail like this...Catwoman sucks mightily on every level – it has the power to unite audience members in the way that any great calamity unites strangers in a common trauma.’

Wow. Walter Chaw from filmfreakcentral certainly lays into this recent comic book hero brought to life on the big screen. Calling the film the ‘ambassador of dreadful’ and giving it zero stars out of four, he echoes the vast majority of reviews for mononymed director Pitof’s box office catastrophe – rated by a Newsweek poll as the worst film of 2004. In contrast, the same poll listed another comic book hero’s silver screen adventure as the best film of 2004: Spider-Man 2.

In a way, the varying fortunes of these two films encapsulate the current state of the Hollywood production line. Bereft of new ideas, it is stuck on the comic book hero treadmill – a first-cousin to the 60s TV series remake machine – churning out silver screen adaptations of Marvel and DC characters at an alarming rate. Only the number of comic book hero films in development outweighs the number of comic book hero films seen recently on our screens.

At this rate it’s likely that the whole comic book movie market will collapse within a year due to over-exploitation, and Catwoman has the dubious honour of actually being canary-woman, her death the first sign that the comic book air is now too dangerous to breathe in Hollywood.

Catwoman’s mostly kitty litter

Catwoman is symptomatic of all that is wrong with the genre. She has little in common with her character’s origins, not even sharing a name; the film is not set in Gotham City, distancing it even further from the franchise this feminine feline sharpened her claws in; her trademark bodysuit has been replaced with a gimp mask and bra while her moral ambiguity has been replaced by two-dimensional good-girlness. Worst of all, it’s obvious that a lot of money went into making Catwoman, but it's equally as obvious that hardly any of it went to the writers.

The question most fans are asking is whether or not Pitof or any of the cast or crew actually read the source material. Speaking to FilmForce Magazine prior to the flick’s release, Catwoman star Halle Berry claimed she had to resist mimicking Eartha Kitt, the star who played Catwoman in the 60s TV series of Batman.

‘The good thing about our movie is we acknowledge all the Catwomen of the past,’ she said. ‘We believe that there are nine Catwomen and I'm just one of them. There's been five already, so I guess I'd be sixth. So I'm my own version.’ The problem here is that Berry’s version is so far from the original that she is unrecognisable; Catwoman is not Dr Who. >>>
