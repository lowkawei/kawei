Spider-Man, Spider-Man, does whatever Catwoman can’t

At the other end of the spectrum, Sam Raimi’s Spider-Man and Spider-Man 2 have been roundly applauded by comic book fans for sticking to the look and feel of the source; in doing so they have also won over the wider film-going community. These films have got it right. Produced by Marvel, the publishers of Stan Lee’s original comic books, they are as much about the Peter Parker character – a believably flawed young man – as they are about the Spider-Man character, and thus tread that fine line between human believability that the audience connects with and super-human impossibility that viewers fantasise about.

Many fans and film critics attribute the Spider-Man films’ success to Raimi, who has the distinction of being a comic fan-boy from way back and who was, therefore, passionately familiar with the original source books (as well as being well-versed in making fantasy films). The first film, for instance, spends almost an hour setting the scene instead of rushing into the bad-guy biffing and kapowing in the first 15 minutes, like so many other over-anxious comic book heroes on screen do. Meanwhile, Raimi’s characters are three dimensional, with Tobey Maguire in the title role a particular revelation.

‘I think the audience likes the character of Peter Parker,’ Raimi told press last year. ‘They like his personal struggle: responsibility versus self-fulfilment. Spider-Man is supposed to show us how to do things – not tell us moral lessons. That’s why he’s the hero. He does the right thing. That was one of the aspects of the comic that’s always been interesting.’

Talking about the sequel in particular, Raimi reveals his deep-seated respect for the original source material: ‘The story I was interested in with Peter Parker was that to use this gift, he had to deny his own personal life. I felt the villain should be someone with an opposing philosophy, who values personal fulfilment over responsibility towards others. It was a good way to throw into relief the basic theme of the story.’

Like the original Catwoman, Raimi’s Spider-Man wrestles with doing what is right vs doing what he wants with his new-found super powers, and the films rely strongly on Stan Lee’s moral message – provided via Uncle Ben – that ‘with great power comes great responsibility’.

Hollywood should heed that message. It has mammoth power to bring other people’s visions to life, but it also has a responsibility not to regurgitate those visions in a mutated and unrecognisable form. >>>
