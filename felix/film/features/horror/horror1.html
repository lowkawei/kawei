The blood-soaked reels of horror film

The horror genre is almost as old as film itself. Pauline de Laveaux discusses the classics and how they thrill.

Slashers, vampires, zombies, demonic possession and Satanism, alien mind control, evil children, cannibalism, werewolves, animals attacking humans, haunted houses – just a few favourite themes of the horror film that have been immortalised throughout the decades.

The horror genre thrives on the enthusiasm of each new generation. It’s been popular since the dawn of film, transcending time and culture. Fear is universal; language barriers don’t exist in horror films as they deliver visceral power. There aren’t too many ways to interpret blood and gore. You either love the adrenaline rush from seeing someone being brutally hacked to death, or you run away screaming and avoid it all together.

However, as the horror catalogue has grown, films like Scream and I Know What You Did Last Summer have complicated this simple division of ‘love it or hate it’. They poke fun at the genre, and appeal to audiences who normally dislike horror films. Now in the post-Scream era, directors have gone one step further to offer the self-referential, teen-comedy-horror flicks like Scary Movie, which some horror enthusiasts argue aren’t really horror at all.

So when you ask a real connoisseur what they think are the films that comprise the canon of their favourite genre, what responses do you get?

Many say that you can’t go past Tobe Hooper’s The Texas Chainsaw Massacre, an absolute cult classic, often referred to as one of the most frightening movies ever made. Its recently released successor prompted many angry forum topics, posing the question: ‘Why remake the original?’

Asking people what they liked about horror, besides the violence, elicited many passionate responses.

A lot of people love the experience; vicariously suffering through the victim, or enjoying the illicit thrill of being the killer. Someone said, ‘I like my horror camp and I like horror that doesn’t take itself seriously,’ – this was a popular sentiment. People like a bit of humour, and people love the voyeuristic thrills that a horror film provides. But they still like to be reminded that they’re only watching a film.

When I prompted people to think of the first horror film that came to their minds, I was slightly surprised to hear movies like Psycho and The Birds. Hitchcock isn’t the first director that springs to my mind when considering horror, but he certainly is a master.

Horror has many spectrums and sub-genres, so I sought my friend’s advice, an ultimate horror buff. When MIFF comes along, he’s been known to choose from the guide by highlighting anything, which says ‘may offend some viewers’ usually due to the high levels of blood and guts.

He said: ‘For me, I love gore, I love maniacs, zombies, lots of blood, possession is great; I really love vampires.’ He ran off a list of some of the pioneers, which included Dario Argento’s distinctly European flavoured Suspiria amongst films like Rosemary’s Baby, The Exorcist, Halloween, Night of the Living Dead, The Shining and The Evil Dead.
