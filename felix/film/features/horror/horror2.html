Blair Witch revives the 90s lull in the horror project

What can be said of the last five years in horror filmmaking? Okay, we’ve had the controversial remakes, the sequels and prequels like The Exorcist: the beginning and films like Jeepers Creepers and Valentine, which didn’t seem to make much of an impression on the scene. We’ve then had the long awaited Freddy vs. Jason which, depending on who you talk to, is one of the coolest horror films of all time, or grounds for the justifiable homicide of director Ronny Yu. But are any of these really classics?

Luckily for modern horror, Eduardo Sanchez and Daniel Myrick directed the uniquely terrifying The Blair Witch Project – unprecedented by any other film.

The premise is very simple, with a lasting effect on audiences. The film opens with a title card announcing that in October of 1994, three student filmmakers disappeared near Burkittsville – previously called Blair; a year later, their footage was found and turned into The Blair Witch Project. No more really needs to be said to introduce this film.

A friend who had seen the movie whilst at film school said that she was part of a viewing experiment when they screened it to the students. The group was divided in two. The first group was told that it was a low-budget film and asked to examine its stylistic techniques, whilst the other group was told that what they were about to see was a documentary filmed by three teenagers, who were now presumed to be dead as a result.

My friend, who was in the latter group, described her hysterically disturbed reaction after the viewing. People had been screaming and crying, until they were told the truth. But even knowing that it is nothing more than a staged play, it is still deeply unsettling – scraping at the nerves. Its simplicity makes the film frighteningly realistic. >>>
