The duality of Woody Allen

‘Let me tell you a story, and you tell me, is it material for a comedy or a tragedy?’ Woody Allen asks in his latest film, Melinda and Melinda. On the eve of its release in Australia, director Allen and star Radha Mitchell discuss comedy, drama, money and aggressive womanising with Paul Fischer in New York.

Woody Allen’s Melinda And Melinda is a tragi-comedy set in Manhattan (isn’t this a description of all Allen’s films?) chronicling a pair of crises that give rein to the funny and serious talents of Radha Mitchell, Will Ferrell, Josh Brolin, Jonny Lee Miller, Amanda Peet and Chloë Sevigny, amongst others. All the usual Allen areas are explored – the fragility of love, marital infidelity, sophisticated romance, the inability to communicate. As a character in the movie puts it, ‘He's despondent, he's desperate, he's suicidal. All the comic elements are in place.’

Mitchell plays both title characters. The Australian actress, whose biggest role to date was alongside Vin Diesel in Pitch Black, acknowledges that she was surprised to be offered the part. ‘It was a little random,’ she chuckles. ‘I don't know how [Allen] got my cell phone number. I had just come back from Australia and I thought it was my mum but it was Woody Allen just checking to see if I wanted to be in his movie!’

Money, money, money

One of the most prolific and acclaimed writer/directors of his generation, the former Allen Konigsberg (yes, that was his real name!) could have given the role to just about anybody he wanted; he has little trouble attracting major stars to his films, even though he is notorious for paying meagre salaries – by Hollywood standards. It’s not entirely because actors are moths drawn to Allen’s reputation, however. The bespectacled director slyly admits that he waits until his chosen stars have nothing else to do.

‘They only work with me if they're between desirable jobs,’ Allen laughs. ‘If I call an actor or actress and Stephen Spielberg or Martin Scorsese is calling them and offering them substantial money, they have no interest in me at all. If they just finished a picture, however, and they’ve earned their ten million dollar salary, and they have nothing to do 'til August, I call them in June; if they like the part, they say “why not?”’

Even though Allen's films continue to be made at a fraction of the cost of the traditional studio film, he often ponders what it would be like to have that $100m budget. ‘I'm making films for a maximum of 14 or 15 million dollars and it's tough,’ he whinges, ‘because there's a lot of things I want to do that I can't do with that kind of money. Any kind of special effects or re-shooting, you can't do it.’

But a Woody Allen film wouldn’t be a Woody Allen film if it had special effects. The director has built his career on the ‘human’ themes of love, relationships, sex and his own various neuroses. Melinda and Melinda is no exception. >>>
