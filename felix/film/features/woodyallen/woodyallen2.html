Woody said

Everybody loves to drop a good Woody Allen quote into the conversation. Here’s a few to get you started:

  1. ‘Eighty percent of success is showing up.’
  2. ‘His lack of education is more than compensated for by his keenly developed moral bankruptcy.’
  3. ‘I can't listen to that much Wagner. I start getting the urge to conquer Poland.’
  4. ‘I was thrown out of college for cheating on the metaphysics exam; I looked into the soul of the boy sitting next to me.’
  5. ‘Is sex dirty? Only if it’s done right.’

Don’t sweat the heavy stuff

‘There have been many times when I've had ideas that I felt would have worked either way, amusingly or as a serious story,’ Allen explains, ‘and in the past I'd always chosen one and gone in that direction. This was an idea which I again thought could make quite a serious story, but could also make a quite funny romantic story. And then it occurred to me: why don't I alternate and see if I could learn something from trying to juxtapose the two. Of course, I learned nothing in trying to do this,’ he adds, laughingly. ‘It was fun to do, but not enlightening.’

Asked about his preference, Allen somewhat surprisingly confesses that it's ‘always fun to write the heavy stuff’. He attributes this to his overindulgence in comic films, saying that ‘it's nice to do something occasionally that's very, very heavy just for the change.’ In fact, the script for Melinda and Melinda was initially heading in this direction, he admits, but when funny man Will Ferrell came on board, Allen went back to his original idea of juxtaposing the light and dark, and in customising the script for Ferrell, the laughs crept back in.

Jewish Comedy?

As Melinda juxtaposes an often bleak tragedy with a typically Allen tone of comedy, an interesting question surfaces: that perhaps the comedy is for Jews and the drama is for Wasps. The director chuckles at the suggestion and agrees to an extent.

‘I don't really think of it that way, but I guess many people think of comedy for Jews all the time. I'm forever being asked “why are all the comedians Jewish?” but I don’t feel that they are. This is a misconception based on the fact that there were many Jewish comedians that came out of the Catskills, but if you look at Bob Hope, Buster Keaton or WC Fields, they weren’t Jewish. Charlie Chaplin was half-Jewish, so which half is the comic half? Peter Sellers, too, was only half-Jewish. I don't think it's a particularly Jewish thing.’

Yet Allen's own comic tone, going back to his genesis as a writer and director, has remained very Jewish, a fact he reflects upon when he concedes that he is constrained, like most authors, to merely write what he knows.

‘I was raised in a Jewish neighbourhood in a Jewish household so naturally my idiom is where I grew up. I've had this conversation with Spike Lee a number of times: I could never write convincingly about a black family and I doubt if he could write convincingly about a Jewish family. When you live every moment of it, it gets into the nuances.’ >>>
