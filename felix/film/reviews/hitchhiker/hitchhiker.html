Hitchhiker’s Guide To The Galaxy
Touchstone Pictures

Unfortunately, Douglas Adams died before he could see what a great movie his book has made. The legendary humorist, who had a heart attack in 2001, wrote the script for The Hitchhiker’s Guide to the Galaxy and has been post-humously credited as executive producer. Honouring his wonderful work, the film shines with sharp comic performances and superb effects, with the guide itself brought to life by similarly wry comic quirks of UK animation group Shynola. For the most part, it is funny in exactly the way it should be.

Following the unusual adventures of upright Brit Arthur Dent (Martin Freeman) on the day the Earth is destroyed to make way for a hyperspace express route, The Hitchhiker’s Guide to the Galaxy takes its name from the number one information resource for wayward interstellar travelers: a book given to Arthur by his alien friend Ford Prefect (Mos Def) seconds after the two of them have hitchhiked off of the doomed planet. Their ensuing adventures include a spaceship of infinite improbability, a quest for the meaning of life and an alcoholic beverage that makes you feel like your brain is being beaten with lemon slices wrapped around a gold brick.

The source material is peerless and perfect, so the biggest responsibility of director Garth Jennings was not to fuck it up. From a questionable musical opening, Jennings quickly hits his stride with a seemingly effortless balance between understated humor and delightfully apt visual representations of Adams’ universe. Martin Freeman delivers the same self-deprecating charm he brought to The Office and Sam Rockwell proves his comic genius again with a fast-talking Zaphod Beeblebrox, but the surprise luminary is Mos Def with an utterly hilarious, energetic and inventive Ford. In a cast of cameo superstardom including John Malkovich, Stephen Fry and Bill Nighy, his performance barely has the room to shine but manages to do it anyway.

The only disappointment in a mostly wonderful film is Alan Rickman’s Marvin, the terminally depressed robot. This fêted malcontent has a biting bitterness in the novel that is missing in Rickman’s doleful translation, which is a serious pity, because Marvin is what you might call an ‘event character’ in the original story. Marvin made quite an impression, and if you’re any kind of Adams fan, you’re likely to miss him. But anyway, forgive the misfire. The rest of the film is a hit.

Simone Ubaldi
