Kingdom Of Heaven
20th Century Fox

Terrifically pompous and awfully confusing, Kingdom of Heaven has the feel of an epic novel awkwardly distilled into plot-driving episodes which hint at but do not properly explain what, exactly, the film is about. Set in 12th century Jerusalem at the end of an uneasy peace between the ousted Saracyn tribes and the occupying Crusaders from England and France, the film at first champions the virtues of a world in which men can put aside their differences and live in harmony and later shows these same men in a grim battle for the city, arguing for a completely different and somewhat cloudy set of virtues. It makes no sense, but it is extraordinary to look at.

Orlando Bloom stars as Balian, a French blacksmith who is reclaimed by his long-lost father, a Baron in service of the king of Jerusalem. Struck by an arrow in a skirmish, the Baron hastily knights his son and sends him, filled with instructions on nobility and wisdom, to take his place in the East. Once there, Balian quickly emerges as a righteous leader and a formidable fighter, who stands with the Lord Tiberius against those French noblemen who would break the peace and begin a war with the Saracyns, claiming it is the will of god. Eventually the King dies and the Kingdom of Heaven is thrown into turmoil, with an epic battle sequence and a couple of rabble rousing speeches by Balian that do nothing to clarify who or what we are supposed to be sympathising with.

Director Ridley Scott has taken a risk on this script, which, aside from being somewhat complicated and unfeasible, is linguistically unforgiving. He’s rewarded with a story that, while obscure, is unlike any we’ve seen before and a landscape and culture that have only seen limited use in dated religious epics. Kingdom of Heaven features some of the most breathtaking visual events in recent cinema, from overhead shots of an Arab army driving thousands of horses in a circular frenzy in the desert to the intimate explosions of fire as the Saracyns catapult Jerusalem. Even in quiet moments, the dust and the camera are mesmerisingly fluid, so that where Gladiator appalled with its heavy-handed machismo and superficial spectacle, Kingdom of Heaven is film of unexpected subtlety. If only the story had a little more room to breathe.

Simone Ubaldi
