Star Wars Episode III: Revenge of the Sith
Lucasfilm

The last two episodes in the new trilogy of the Star Wars saga were disappointing, to say the least. The bland and soulless The Phantom Menace, with its blatant rip-off of the famous chariot race from Ben Hur, was easily the worst film in the series. The Attack Of The Clones was more exciting, but it still missed the mark. It seemed that while special effects technology and CGI expanded enormously in the quarter century since the original Star Wars burst on to our screens, George Lucas’ storytelling abilities halted.

At least the eagerly awaited final chapter in this new trilogy remedies many of the problems that bogged down the previous two films – internally, there’s less politics here as Lucas crams in more action and light sabre duels, a decision that will undoubtedly please fans. (The director admits, however, that Revenge Of The Sith can be interpreted as a political allegory for our times.) It spoils little to reveal that this film’s primary objective is to fill in the back story of how eager young Jedi apprentice Anakin Skywalker (again played by Hayden Christensen, who seems less wooden here than before) falls under the spell of the dark side of the force and eventually becomes Darth Vader.

The Revenge Of The Sith takes up the story three years after the clone wars, and begins with a final confrontation with the villainous Count Dooku (Christopher Lee). Eager to be accepted by the Jedi council, Anakin falls under the spell of the evil, megalomaniacal Chancellor Palpatine (a suitably malevolent Ian McDiarmid) whose promises of unnatural powers seduce him. Anakin helps Palpatine destroy the Jedi council, paving the way for the Empire to dominate the galaxy. Meanwhile, fearing for her safety, Padme (Natalie Portman) turns to Obi-wan Kenobi (Ewan McGregor) to ensure the safety of her babies, Luke and Leia. The surviving Jedis, Obi-wan and Yoda, flee into exile, awaiting the time when they can emerge from hiding and take on the Empire and restore peace to the galaxy.

There are some amazing special effects, and some superbly staged action sequences that will take your breath away. But, for all that, The Revenge Of The Sith still lacks that sense of awe and wonderment of the very first time you saw the original Star Wars on the screen! The dialogue is occasionally clunky, and the on-going love story between Anakin and Princess Padme lacks any real passion. TheRevenge Of The Sith also provides tantalising glimpses of familiar characters like Chewbacca, while R2D2 and C3PO begin to develop their unique personalities.

Greg King
