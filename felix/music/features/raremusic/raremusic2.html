Should rare music be sold on the internet?

Some music store owners are not in favour of the internet’s role in the rare music trade. Melbourne’s Greville Records take a strictly purist approach to trade. Greville’s well-informed staff are there to help if you can’t find what you want in the store’s impressive collection. But they regard buying music as a direct and personal experience – not a journey through cyberspace. You can’t visit Greville Records online because ‘we have a door at the front of our shop.’

Even stores who have tossed their collections into the internet fray remain wary of online rare music sales. Bill Morton, owner of Melbourne’s The Last Record Store, says a single high-priced sale on eBay increases prices for everyone. To get an item’s true value, ‘get the price on eBay and then divide by about eleven,’ Bill jokes. ‘Prices are artificially inflated.’

He also thinks merchants who once sold cheaply to collectors know they can make more money online from superstar DJs who take pleasure in overpaying for rare items.

Bill acknowledges that there’s great stuff available in cyberspace, but urges caution. ‘Too many people think they’re running a museum, not a shop. Check around before buying.’
