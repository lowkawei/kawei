Acre
In God’s Car There Are No Spare Seats
Route Note Records

Once I hit my sister in the eyeball with a ping-pong ball and it filled completely with blood, and she had to be rushed to hospital. Despite this traumatic memory (I thought I’d permanently blinded her), I love the first track on Acre’s new album. You might have heard it on the radio; it starts with a sampled recording of a snappy ping-pong game which blends into a rollicking beat complete with xylophone, banjo and harmonica stylings.

It’s an apt signature to an album that’s refreshingly inventive, entertaining and well crafted in terms of song writing and production. Spare Seats is cruisey, bluesy, folksy stuff with a wide range of instruments contributing to a sound not quite like anything I’ve heard before. There aren’t too many moments of irritation or intrusiveness; it’s a dynamic, wandering listen that keeps you interested all the way through. I like playing it at high volume while doing the washing up, or while being in traffic.

The bio claims the group’s influences include Neil Young, The Church, Wilco and Tom Waits. I pay that and up the ante by adding Pavement to the list.

This four-piece come from Byron Bay and they formed six years ago. This is their second album. It's very impressive. You should go to your change jar and scrape together enough coins to purchase it immediately. Put it on before someone suggests something crap to play at a party. And then they’ll say, “Who is this? They’re awesome”, and you can say, “Oh, they’re just this Byron band I heard about, they’re really good” and everyone will think you’re ahead of time. Just like when you were listening to John Butler before anyone ever heard of him. You dig?

Philip Kaberry
